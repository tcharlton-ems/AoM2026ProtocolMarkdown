<h1 id="appendix-k-air-medicalhelicopter-safety">Appendix K: Air Medical/Helicopter Safety</h1>
<h2 id="ky-all">KY-ALL</h2>
<p><strong>I. INTRODUCTION:</strong></p>
<p>A. Landing zone and Safety. Without exception, safety is air medical service’s top priority.</p>
<p><strong>II. Requesting a helicopter</strong></p>
<p>A. Private Citizens - call 9-1-1.</p>
<p>B. Police, fire and EMS - Request a helicopter through the appropriate agency, such as your dispatch center, with the following information:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Location cross street</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Location LAT/LONG coordinates</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Any prominent features at the scene</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Your call-back number</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Scene radio frequency and CTCSS tone.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Call sign of LZ (landing zone) Command. One person should be designated to coordinate LZ setup and communicate with responding aircraft. This person should not be involved with patient care.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Weather, including low ceilings, poor visibility, icing, and high winds.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Patient status such as number, condition, age, approximate patient weight, mechanism of injury, and hazards.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>ALWAYS RELAY ANY INFORMATION PERTAINING TO HAZMAT TO THE COMMUNICATIONS CENTER WHEN REQUESTING AIR MEDICAL SERVICE.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>C. Notify all involved communications centers if any other air medical service has been contacted and the status of that agency. Always inform all communications centers if other aircraft are anticipated to be in the area.</p>
<p><strong>III. LZ details</strong></p>
<p>A. The preferred landing zone is 100 x 100 feet.</p>
<p>B. Important Tips</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Never approach the aircraft until instructed to do so and only as instructed by the pilot or flight crew aboard.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Approach angles over obstacles should be less than 20 degrees</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Always keep LZ clear of people and other potential hazards</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Under no circumstances should you ever approach the aircraft from the rear</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>IV. Landing Zone Setup</strong></p>
<p>A. Set up the LZ as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>SIZE should be 100 feet by 100 feet</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>LEVEL: Select a LZ as level as possible (minimal slope)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>LANDING SURFACE: Select a hard surface, grassy surface, or hard- packed snow. Avoid loose dirt, dust, or powder snow.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>CLEAR OVERHEAD free of obstructions such as wires, antennas, or poles</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>CLEAR AREA free of debris, large rocks, posts, stumps, vehicles, people, animals, and other hazards</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>MARK THE AREA clearly using five weighted cones or beacons, one at each corner of the LZ and one on the side that wind is coming from</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>SELECT AN ALTERNATE LZ. Plan for an alternate LZ because the pilot may determine your LZ to be unsafe.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>HAZMAT: Always relay any information pertaining to HAZMAT to the communications center when requesting air medical service. Always inform the pilot and medical crew of HAZMAT. When selecting a LZ find a site at least 1/4 to 1 mile UPWIND from the incident depending on the type and materials involved. Avoid low areas where vapors may collect. The patient must be removed from the hot zone. All patients must be decontaminated PRIOR to flight.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>B. When the helicopter is overhead</p>
<ol>
<li>Air medical service will establish radio contact on the assigned frequency with LZ Command three to five minutes out. Describe the following:</li>
</ol>
<p>a. LZ location</p>
<p>b. Lighting</p>
<p>c. Hazards</p>
<p>d. Overhead wires, including wires along the approach path to the LZ</p>
<p>e. Obstructions</p>
<p>f. Slope</p>
<p>g. Surface conditions</p>
<p>h. Wind direction and speed if known</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Maintain radio contact at all times until the helicopter has landed, loaded, and departed the area.</li>
</ol>
<p>C. Night Landing Zone</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>DO NOT SHINE LIGHTS DIRECTLY AT THE HELICOPTER</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Set up night landing zones with five strobes or other secured lights. Do not use cones, flares,</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>or tape to mark the site.</p>
<ol start="3">
<li>
<p>Emergency vehicles may be parked so their headlights intersect the middle of the landing site and/or parked underneath wires to mark them. Turn strobes of emergency vehicles off as the aircraft approaches.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lights may be shown onto poles indicating wires between the poles</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Night landing zones always require good communications, lighting, and alertness</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Turn off all emergency lights after aircraft has started approach</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>One strobe should be on the side that the wind is coming from</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If no strobes are available mark with other lighting systems</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If no other portable lights are available, cross headlight beams into the wind at the center of the landing zone</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>V. Helicopter Utilization Criteria for Scene Response</strong></p>
<p>A. Purpose:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Air Medical Services (AMS) are a valuable, yet limited resource in the Commonwealth. It is important that Emergency Medical Service personnel utilize consistent and appropriate criteria when requesting an air medical service for assistance with patient care and transport.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The following represents a combination of the current criteria in use throughout the state. These criteria are consistent with national AMS utilization criteria. It is important that review of appropriate helicopter utilization be a part of EMS training, as well as a component of the agency and regional level retrospective quality assurance process.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>B. Criteria:</p>
<ol>
<li>The helicopter is an air ambulance and an essential part of the EMS system. It may be considered in situations wherein:</li>
</ol>
<p>a. The use of the helicopter would speed a patient’s arrival to the hospital capable of providing definitive care and this is felt to be significant to the patient’s condition, or;</p>
<p>b. If specialized services offered by the air medical service would benefit the patient prior to arrival at the hospital.</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>The following criteria should be used when considering use of an air medical service:</li>
</ol>
<p>a. The patient’s condition is a “life or limb” threatening situation demanding intensive multidisciplinary treatment and care. This may include but not be limited to:</p>
<p>i. Patients with physical findings defined in the adult and pediatric major trauma protocols (see attached)</p>
<p>ii. Critical burn patients (see attached)</p>
<p>iii. Critically ill medical patients requiring care at a specialized center to include, but not be limited to acute stroke or ST elevation MI.</p>
<p>iv. Patients in cardiac arrest who are not hypothermic should be excluded from these criteria</p>
<ol start="3">
<li>
<p>Dispatch, Police, Fire or EMS will evaluate the situation/condition and if necessary, may place the helicopter on standby.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The helicopter may be requested to respond to the scene when:</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>a. ALS personnel request the helicopter.</p>
<p>b. BLS personnel request the helicopter, when ALS is delayed or unavailable.</p>
<p>c. In the absence of an EMS agency, any emergency service may request the helicopter, if it is felt to be medically necessary.</p>
<ol start="5">
<li>
<p>When EMS arrive, they should assess the situation. If the MOST HIGHLY TRAINED EMS PERSONNEL ON THE SCENE determine, that the helicopter is not needed, it should be cancelled as soon as possible.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When use of air medical services is not specifically defined by the protocol, the on scene EMS provider should establish communication with medical control to discuss the situation with the on line physician.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Air medical services may be considered in situations where the patient is inaccessible by other means or, if utilization of existing ground transport services threatens to overwhelm the local EMS system.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The destination facility will be determined by the AMS crew based upon medical appropriateness with consideration for patient preference and on line medical direction, in compliance with regional protocols.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An EMS service should not wait on the scene or delay transport waiting for the helicopter to arrive. If the patient is packaged and ready for transport, the EMS service should initiate transport to the hospital and reassign the landing zone. The helicopter may intercept with an ambulance during transport at an alternate- landing site.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>THIS IS A GUIDELINE AND IS NOT INTENDED TO SPECIFICALLY DEFINE EVERY CONDITION IN WHICH AIR MEDICAL SERVICES SHOULD BE REQUESTED. GOOD CLINICAL JUDGEMENT SHOULD BE USED AT ALL TIMES.</p>
<p>C. Transfer of Patient Care, Documentation and Quality Assurance:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>As with other instances where care of a patient is transferred, it is expected that all patient related information, assessment findings and treatment will be communicated to the flight crew.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>At the completion of the EMS call, all of the details of the response, including, but not limited to all patient related information, assessment findings and treatment must be documented.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>As with all EMS responses, helicopter utilization, the treatment and transportation of patients will be reviewed as a part of a Quality Assurance process.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>VI. Guidelines for Helicopter Utilization Criteria for Scene Response</strong></p>
<p>A. ADULT MAJOR TRAUMA</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>GCS less than or equal to 13</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Respiratory Rate less than 10 or more than 29 breaths per minute</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pulse rate is less than 50 or more than 120 beats per minute</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Systolic blood pressure is less than 90mmHg</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Penetrating injuries to head, neck, torso or proximal extremities</p>
</li>
<li></li>
<li>
<p>Two or more suspected proximal long bone fractures</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Suspected flail chest</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Suspected spinal cord injury or limb paralysis</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Amputation (except digits)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Suspected pelvic fracture</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Open or depressed skull fracture</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>B. PEDIATRIC MAJOR TRAUMA</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Pulse greater than normal range for patient’s age</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Systolic blood pressure below normal range</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Respiratory status inadequate (central cyanosis, respiratory rate low for the child’s age, capillary refill time greater than two seconds)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Glasgow coma scale less than 14</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Penetrating injuries of the trunk, head, neck, chest, abdomen or groin</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Two or more proximal long bone fractures</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Flail chest</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Combined system trauma that involves two or more body systems, injuries or major blunt trauma to the chest or abdomen</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Spinal cord injury or limb paralysis</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Amputation (except digits)</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>C. CRITICAL BURNS</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Greater than 20% Body Surface Area (BSA) second or third degree burns</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Evidence of airway/facial burns</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Circumferential extremity burns</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>**Note that for patients with burns and coexisting trauma, the traumatic injury should be considered the first priority and the patient should be triaged to the closest appropriate trauma center for initial stabilization.</p>
<p>D. CRITICAL MEDICAL CONDITIONS</p>
<ol>
<li>Suspected Acute Stroke</li>
</ol>
<p>a. Positive Cincinnati Pre-hospital Stroke Scale</p>
<p>b. Total prehospital time (time from when the patient’s symptoms and/or signs first began to when the patient is expected to arrive at the Stroke Center) is less than two (2) hours.</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Suspected Acute Myocardial Infarction</li>
</ol>
<p>a. Chest pain, Shortness of breath or other symptoms typical of a cardiac event</p>
<p>b. EKG findings of o ST elevation 1mm or more in 2 or more contiguous leads OR LBBB (QRS duration &gt;.12msec and Q wave in V1 or V2</p>
