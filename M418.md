<h1 id="hyperkalemia">Hyperkalemia</h1>
<h2 id="all">ALL</h2>
<p>I. Inclusion Criteria</p>
<p>A. Patientâ€™s age is 16 years or older.</p>
<p>B. Symptomatic hyperkalemia with EKG changes.</p>
<p>II. Protocol</p>
<h2 id="emt">EMT</h2>
<p>A. Maintain airway and administer oxygen to correct hypoxia &lt;95%.</p>
<p>B. Place on cardiac monitor.</p>
<p>C. Obtain diagnostic if able and transmit.</p>
<h2 id="medic">Medic</h2>
<p>D. Obtain IV/IO access.</p>
<p>E. Treat with the following:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Calcium per T714 Calcium Administration.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate 1 mEq/kg IV/IO.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Albuterol/duoneb nebulized continuously (may discontinue with EKG improvement).</p>
</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="all-1">ALL</h2>
<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<p>A. Hyperkalemia is the serum potassium above the reference range of 5.5 mmol/L that can lead to severe cardiac, hemodynamic, and metabolic dysfunction. Signs and symptoms of severe hyperkalemia include:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Peaked T waves, QRS &gt; 0.12 ms, +/- hypotension</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Bicarbonate and calcium can precipitate in same line, therefore, must be given with adequate flushing of the line or in a separate line.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>B. Consider these treatments early in known end-stage renal disease (ESRD) that are in cardiac arrest.</p>
<ol>
<li>In these situations, substitute Calcium chloride 20mg/kg (max 1000mg) IVP.</li>
</ol>
